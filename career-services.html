Students increasingly report that they attend higher education to get a job or advance in their career. For universities, helping students find good jobs after college is critical to helping them repay any debt they incur and build a loyal alumni base that will support the university. Reinventing a university’s Career Services department so that it can fulfill these demands is imperative. Our team created this guide to help universities design and implement an effective Career Services strategy. We start with the overview of key components and self-evaluation checklist, then go to vendor landscape and notable examples of innovation, and conclude with recommendations and further resources.
1.Key Components
The following are some of the key components of a good strategy to help a university’s Career Services office innovate. The list isn’t mutually exclusive; universities can mix and match from the different options. More actionable advice follows in the Recommendations section.
Career Coaching
  • Career Assessment - The evaluation of a student’s skills, interests, and abilities is an integral first step to putting students on a strategic and successful career track.
  • Career Goal Setting - Using the results of a career assessment to develop a student’s short- and long-term career goals, as well as the strategies to accomplish them, this step helps students stay motivated and course correct when necessary.
  • Career Action Plan - This could take the form of a timeline, spreadsheet, essay, mind map, flowchart, or progression map – whichever appeals most to the student and keeps them engaged in their goals.
  • Career Check-Up - Students occasionally change their career plans. It’s important that they can leverage resources from Career Services to adjust their plan as needed.
Job Search Skills Training
  • Resume and Cover Letter Writing - Career Services should host workshops to help students develop critical writing skills to improves their job prospects. Offering mMeetings with a career coach or industry mentor to review resumes will also helps a great deal, as well.
  • Mock Interviews - Feedback from mock interviews helps students improve their interviewing skills in a low-stakes environment. This scaffolds students into their job search and can often be the difference between landing a great job and unemployment.
E-portfolios and Profiles
  • E-portfolio - A digital repository for students to showcase their work, experiences, skills, and accomplishments, e-portfolios act as an expanded resume. They can include videos, writing samples, software, research projects, and much more.
  • Social Media Profiles - Sites like LinkedIn, Twitter, and GitHub enable students to create a polished professional image. They also help students connect with potential employers, promote their experience and expertise, and build a network of influencers in their field.
Experiential Learning
  • Internships - Internships give students the ability to apply classroom learning in a professional environment, develop relationships with industry professionals, and build a network of contacts.
  • Intern for a Day - These are the internship versions of “hack-a-thons.” They allowallowing students to pair with an industry professional for a day, and perform the tasks of a real job. They are great network,ing opportunities and sometimes they even result help students land in a fullreal internship or job.
  • On-Campus Employment - On-campus employment is usually part-time and is occasionally accompanied by a tuition reimbursement program. It’s an excellent way to provide real-world experience under faculty and staff mentorship.
Networking and Events
  • Career Meetups - Informal gatherings thattypically led by career coaches typically lead, meetups shouldmay involve alumni and employers willingwho have volunteered to help students with their careers exploration. They should focus on topics essential to career success.
  • Informational Interviews - Informational interviews take place with professionals in a particular field to provide students with an insider’s perspective on a careerthat will benefit their continued job search and professional growth. They can take place in person, online or by phone, and colleges can organize them for students with their alums., and are conducted with professionals in a particular field to learn about their expertise, career path and professional insights.
  • Student-Alumni Networking - Leverage your alumni. Building relationships Students making connections to, and maintaining relationships with, alumni is the most effective source of new employment opportunities for graduating students.
Employer Partnerships
  • Industry Mentoring - Connecting experienced industry professionals, oftentimes alumni, with students for one-on-one career guidance increases students’ employability in a chosen field.
  • Part-Time Faculty Members from Industry - Experienced industry professionals who continue to work in their field, while also teaching as a member of the faculty, serve as industry ambassadors and great career resources for students.
  • Industry Projects - Projects submitted by industry partners that, to be completed by students tackle, serve as a win-/win for both parties, with students obtaining valuable experience and partners getting quality work at a low cost.
  • Employer Office Hours - Regularly scheduled on-campus visits, usually several hours per week, help employers connect with students and build a strong, positive organizational brand.
  • Resume Book - When students upload their resume to a Resume Book, they allow interested employers to view their resume and contact them directly about jobs openings, information sessions, and interviews.
  • Employer Services - Help employers increase visibility and maximize recruiting efforts on-campus by offering them an Employer Services package. The package might include a logo on the Career Center homepage, resume book access, or other helpful branding and recruiting tools.
Employer Marketing
  • Branding - Universities must create a unique brand image in order to persuade employers to recruit from the institution.
  • Brand Positioning - Brand positioning establishes competitive advantages over competing universities and showcasesexhibits market niches that appeal to particular employers or industries.
  • Career Services Newsletter - A recurring mass email, sent to employers and recruiters and containing Career Services updates, helps keep partners engaged and interested in recruiting from a university.
  • Career Services Blog - Blogs, located on a dedicated section of a Career Services website, should be written by various stakeholders and range between 500-700 words. They should focus on exciting updates and stateistics interesting to employers.
  • Social Media Profiles - Sites like LinkedIn, Twitter, and Facebook enable a university to build its brand image, provide employers and recruiters with the important information, and promote interesting facts and statistics.
Outcome Tracking
  • Career Outcomes - Information about how graduates of the institution are performing professionally after graduation helps build an institution’s brand image and assists future students in making informed choices about enrollment.

Bentley University
Implemented the HIRE Education Program, a customized career development plan

HIRE Education encompasses the hundreds of programs Career Services offers to help Bentley’s students develop the skills and contacts that pave the way for professional success. Its customized, four-year career development plan is designed to help students during every phase of their Bentley career to prepare for professional success. HIRE Education has four key stages: Explore, Experiment, Experience, and Excel. Each one ties directly to a year in school. Students can engage at different times or revisit certain parts of the program when they wish. The goal is for students to acquire lifelong professional development skills. All stages of HIRE Education develop the core aspects of students’ job search strategy: creating a resume, conducting informational interviews, networking, developing an “elevator pitch,” practicing interviews, and more. Bentley was ranked #4 on the Princeton Review’s list of “Best Career Center Services” in 2014.
  • http://careeredge.bentley.edu/hire-education/
The University of Waterloo
A global leader in co-operative education, the Waterloo co-op program has a student employment rate of 97%

The University of Waterloo operates the largest post secondary co-op program of its kind in the world. Co-operative education combines academic studies with work experience. A co-op program allows students to gain up to two years of relevant work experience. Study terms alternate with work terms to create a study-work sequence. Study-work sequences vary between programs. A work term is usually four months long, but some programs have double work terms that are eight months long. Waterloo offers more than 122 co-op programs, and more than 60% of undergraduate students are enrolled in the co-op program.
  • https://uwaterloo.ca/co-operative-education/
Northeastern University
Each year more than 7,000 students in more than 90 countries are participating in a co-op.

Students in Northeastern’s co-op program alternate between semesters of academic study and semesters of full-time employment in positions related to their academic and career interests in the U.S. or globally. Students start with a required co-op course that prepares them to succeed in their co-op experiences. Their first co-op opportunity typically begins in the second semester of their sophomore year, and students can complete up to three co-ops in the course of a five-year program, or, if they choose, up to two co-ops in the course of a four-year program. Northeastern provides a broad-based network of support that helps students prepare for and get the most from its co-op program, including an academic advisor, a co-op coordinator, and, if applicable, an international co-op counselor.
  • http://www.northeastern.edu/coop/students/overview/
California State University Northridge
The META Lab employs and engages students in a real-world working environment

Based at California State University, META (Metador Emerging Technology and Arts) Lab is an academic unit that is comprised of faculty, staff, and students. Students take a leadership role within the organization, and faculty mentors and staff support them. Participants of the META Lab work on web-based applications, as well as creative strategies and design solutions, within an interdisciplinary setting. Students in the fields of computer science, interface design, user experience, business/marketing, social media, and other related fields are encouraged to discover and participate in META Lab as an extension of their academic program. Students engage in activities that correlate with their curriculum.
  • http://www.csun.edu/academic-affairs/meta-lab
  • https://www.metalab.csun.edu/
Babson College
The Babson Employer in Residence Program and Alumni Industry Adviser (AIA) Program finds new ways of connecting students to employers

Babson’s Centers for Career Development hosts approximately 15 employer-in-residence sessions each semester. This allows students to meet with an employer individually. The meeting’s purpose can be for a resume critique, cover letter review, mock interview, or to just have a general career conversation.
  • http://www.babson.edu/student-life/career-development/undergraduate/recruiting/Pages/Ways-to-Meet-Employers.aspx
Ithaca College
Engages current students as Peer Career Advisors to take part in career services

Peer Career Advisors (PCAs) are student volunteers who play a valuable role in the Career Services office. They are trained to assist fellow IC students with the following career topics: resume and cover letter editing and development, job and internship searching, interview preparation, graduate school search and application process, and using Ithaca College’s online resources such as eRecruiting, the Alumni Directory, and Career Shift.
  • http://www.ithaca.edu/sacl/careers/pcas/
University of Florida
Replaced its long annual reports with dynamic infographics

The University of Florida’s annual reports highlight its wide-range of activities each fiscal year. In support of its "Going Green" philosophy, the Career Resource Center has made a concerted effort to reduce its overall carbon footprint and nowadays produces an online version of its annual report. This enables it to make its data more visible for its stakeholders.
  • http://www.crc.ufl.edu/aboutus/AboutReports.html

5.Issues
In this section we present key debates in the field of Career Services by illustrating some of the most common arguments you might hear on both sides. Of course, innovation is rarely black and white. Innovators take the perspectives of all key stakeholders into account, no matter how much they conflict, and work to develop solutions that bring people together and moves everyone forward as partners.
Job placement is the responsibility of
institutions VS. students
“Employment is now one of the top 4 reasons students attend college. They are paying hard-earned money, and often going into debt, on the promise of a good job. It’s the university’s job to make that a reality.”
“If you don't have self-motivation and an internal locus of control it doesn't matter what they teach you. Employers hire the person, not the education.”
Internship placement is the responsibility of
institutions VS. students
“Universities can bring to bear significant resources to create internship opportunities for students. Without these resources, undergraduates are at a huge disadvantages in the job market, even when it comes to internships.”
“Is a student really being prepared for the workforce if they are not left to fend for themselves in the job market? Throw them in the deep end – it’s the best way to learn how to swim.”
Career development training should be
mandatory VS. optional
“Career development is too important to leave to chance. Workshops are critical to job placement, and should be required for all undergraduates.”
“The number of courses an institution can make mandatory is limited. They shouldn’t be wasted on things like workshops, something a student should be motivated to do anyway.”
Career outcomes should be
public VS. private
“Sunlight is one of the most important elements to growth. It may not be pretty at first, but a public accounting will lead to better outcomes.”
“Full transparency is often helpful, but it can be harmful too. Without the right context, outcome statistics can cause the public, and subsequently the university, to focus on the wrong things.”
6.Recommendations
Define your Career Services strategy.
  • Establish long-term objectives and metrics.
  • Allocate financial resources for small pilots and experiments.
Invest in Career Coaching.
  • Develop Career Action Plans for each undergraduate.
  • Offer Career Check-ups to undergraduates who are not satisfied with their career path.
Train job search skills.
  • Organize resume and cover letter writing workshops and mock interviews.
  • Engage alumni in the interview training process.
Embrace e-portfolios and social media profiles as career development tools.
  • Choose and implement an e-portfolio platform.
  • Make e-portfolios and professional social media profiles a standard element of the student experience.
Provide experiential learning opportunities to students.
  • Implement an Intern-for-a-Day program with alumni and key industry partners.
  • Develop industry partnerships that provide regular internship opportunities for undergraduates.
  • Increase the average number of internships students are taking; aim for 2-3 per undergraduate student.
  • Create student-led organizations and studios that provide undergraduates with valuable work experience.
Organize networking and partnership events.
  • Coordinate large-scale events for connecting students to employers.
  • Coordinate student-alumni networking events.
Develop employer partnerships.
  • Engage alumni in the career development process as mentors.
  • Provide the opportunity for increasing employers’ presence on campus through Employer Office Hours.
  • Establish an Industry Projects program and invite employers to participate
  • Create course tracks specific to relevant employers and industries.
Invest in employer marketing.
  • Organize marketing campaigns for prospective employers.
  • Distribute a Career Services newsletter on a regular basis.
  • Create a Career Services blog and social media profiles targeted at employers.
  • Track prospective employers in a CRM system.
Organize outcome tracking by defining Key Performance Indicators (KPIs).
  • Identify the metrics that would measure the career and personal success of graduates
  • Establish a process to collect valuable pre-graduation employment data
  • Establish a process to collect post-graduation employment and personal information.
  • Create a dashboard for the Career Leader listing select KPIs
